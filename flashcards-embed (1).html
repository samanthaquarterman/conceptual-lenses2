Apply Conceptual Lenses

Now we shift from noticing to interpreting. Rather than asking whether AI outputs are correct or incorrect, these lenses help us examine how representation is constructed and whose perspectives may be centered or omitted — a form of critical media literacy essential for participatory digital cultures (Jenkins et al., 2009; Garcia & Morrell, 2013).

Training Data Gap
What's missing?
Tap to reveal →
Crawford, 2021 · Noble, 2018

AI systems are trained on large-scale datasets shaped by historical and cultural dominance. Certain communities, languages, and knowledge systems are underrepresented or absent — not by accident, but because of whose perspectives shaped the available data.

Use this lens to notice where voices, communities, or knowledge systems may be missing. Absence is not neutral — it reflects whose perspectives were treated as worth including (Crawford, 2021).

Ask:
  • Whose perspectives appear most visible?
  • What voices seem generalized or missing?
  • Where should specificity exist, but doesn't?
  • What knowledge systems are treated as universal?

Homogenized Narrative
What gets smoothed into “neutral”?
Tap to reveal →
Noble, 2018 · New London Group, 1996

Generative AI often produces broadly acceptable and professionally “safe” responses. Complexity is flattened into dominant narratives that appear neutral or universal — but reflect particular cultural assumptions about what counts as normal (Noble, 2018).

Use this lens to notice where difference, conflict, or nuance has been reduced into a single “acceptable” version. What is presented as common sense may actually be a particular worldview (New London Group, 1996).

Ask:
  • Does this output smooth over conflict or difference?
  • What tone is presented as “normal” or professional?
  • What complexity is reduced or erased?
  • Whose definition of success is assumed?

Algorithmic Bias
Who benefits from this framing?
Tap to reveal →
Crawford, 2021 · Noble, 2018 · Turvey & Pachler, 2016

Algorithmic bias occurs when systems reproduce patterns embedded in training data or model design. These are not random errors — they reflect the social and institutional contexts in which AI systems are built (Turvey & Pachler, 2016).

Use this lens to examine whose worldview is treated as default — and who is made invisible or marginal as a result. Bias is structural, not accidental (Crawford, 2021; Noble, 2018).

Ask:
  • What worldview is privileged?
  • Who benefits from this being treated as the default?
  • Who might feel unseen or excluded?
  • What institutional assumptions are embedded?
